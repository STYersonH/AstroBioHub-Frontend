In short: Not all cholesterol plaques in the carotid arteries are equally dangerous. This study used advanced MRI (4D Flow MRI) to measure the "frictional force" of blood and found that high-risk plaques experience much greater stress.

When a carotid artery (in the neck) is moderately blocked (30% to 70%), doctors don't always know whether to treat it aggressively (surgery) or conservatively (medication). They need a way to tell if a plaque is "high-risk" (prone to rupture and cause a stroke) or "low-risk".

ðŸ”¬ How Did They Study It?

  • They analyzed 30 patients with plaques in their carotid arteries.
  • They used a special MRI (4D Flow MRI) that allows them to see and measure blood flow in 3D throughout the entire cardiac cycle.
  • They measured the Wall Shear Stress (WSS), which is basically the frictional force that blood exerts on the artery walls as it flows past.
  • They classified plaques as "high-risk" (if they had already caused a small stroke visible in the brain) and "low-risk".

ðŸ“Š Key Findings: The Force Matters!

  • High-risk plaques experienced significantly higher wall shear stress (WSS) than low-risk plaques.
  • This force difference was more noticeable during diastole (when the heart relaxes) than during systole (when the heart pumps).
  • The main force was exerted in the direction of blood flow (axial WSS), which was much stronger than the sideways force (circumferential WSS).
  • The 3D WSS (the combination of all forces) was the best indicator, showing clear differences at every single time point of the heartbeat.

âœ… Conclusion and Clinical Application

Elevated wall shear stress (WSS), especially during diastole and in the axial direction, could be a warning sign that a plaque is unstable and dangerous, even if it doesn't cause a severe blockage.

Measuring this force with 4D Flow MRI can give doctors crucial information to decide on the best treatment and potentially prevent a stroke in patients with moderate stenosis.
