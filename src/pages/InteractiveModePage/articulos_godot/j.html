In short: The European eel is a critically endangered species. A study in the Mar Menor lagoon (Spain), a key habitat for them, reveals that these fish are accumulating high levels of dangerous chemical pollutants, showing signs of genetic damage and posing a risk to human consumption.

ðŸŽ¯ The Problem: A Critical Habitat Under Threat

The European eel spends much of its life in coastal habitats before migrating to reproduce. The Mar Menor is one of the most important, but it suffers from heavy pollution due to intensive agriculture, urban development, and historical mining waste. Contamination is a suspected cause of the eel population's decline.

ðŸ”¬ What Was Analyzed in the Eels?

Scientists studied eels from the Mar Menor to understand the impact of pollution:

  • Toxic Accumulation: They analyzed the muscle tissue of the eels for persistent organic pollutants, such as PCBs, DDTs, and both current-use and banned pesticides (like chlorpyrifos).
  • Biological Warning Signs (Biomarkers): They measured sublethal effects, like DNA damage in blood cells (genotoxicity) and stress on the nervous system.

ðŸ“Š Key Findings: A Toxic Cocktail

  • The eels were exposed to high levels of contaminants banned decades ago, as well as more recent pesticides. For the first time, residues of chlorpyrifos and pendimethalin were detected in eels.
  • Some individual eels exceeded the maximum levels of PCBs permitted by the EU for human consumption.
  • A high frequency of micronuclei was found in their red blood cells, a clear sign that their genetic material was being damaged (genotoxic effect).
  • The youngest and smallest eels appeared to be the most contaminated, possibly due to a "growth dilution" effect in larger individuals.

âœ… Conclusion: A Dual Health Warning

The eels in the Mar Menor are exposed to toxic and carcinogenic chemicals. This poses a twofold threat:

  1. To the species: Genetic damage and other sublethal effects can harm their health and reproductive ability, further contributing to their decline.
  2. To public health: The high concentration of some contaminants makes them unsafe for consumption, yet regulations are lacking for many of the chemicals found.

More research and monitoring are urgently needed to protect this endangered species as well as environmental and public health.
