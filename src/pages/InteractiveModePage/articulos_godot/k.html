In short: Being in space accelerates muscle degeneration, a process similar to aging. Scientists sent a "muscle-on-a-chip" to the International Space Station (ISS) to study this phenomenon and discovered that muscle cells from young and older adults react very differently to microgravity.

ðŸŽ¯ The Problem: Muscle Atrophy in Space and on Earth

Astronauts lose up to 30% of their muscle mass in just one month in space. This process is very similar to sarcopenia, the muscle loss associated with aging on Earth. Understanding what happens in space could help us fight both problems.

ðŸ”¬ The Experiment: A Gym for Cells on the ISS

Researchers created 3D mini human muscles ("myobundles") on a chip using cells from two groups of donors:

  • A group of young, active donors.
  • Another group of old, sedentary donors.

They sent these chips to the ISS. Up there, some muscles received electrical stimulation (like a mini-workout), while others did not. They then analyzed changes in their strength and gene expression.

ðŸ“Š Key Findings: Age and "Exercise" Make a Difference

  • Less Strength in Space: All muscles contracted with less force in microgravity and lost a key protein for slow-twitch fibers (myosin-7).
  • Youth Responds Better: The muscles from young donors that received electrical stimulation showed an improvement in their mitochondria (the cells' "power plants") while in space.
  • Older Muscles, Less Responsive: The muscles from older donors did not show this mitochondrial improvement, being less responsive to the "exercise."
  • Researchers identified 86 specific muscle-aging genes that were altered differently by spaceflight in young vs. old muscles, related to inflammation and cellular stress.

âœ… Conclusion: Space as a Magnifying Glass for Aging

Microgravity affects muscles differently depending on age, accelerating age-like degenerative processes. However, electrical stimulation proved to be a promising countermeasure, especially for younger individuals.

These "on-a-chip" experiments are key to developing therapies that can protect astronauts on long missions and, at the same time, combat sarcopenia here on Earth.
