In short: In 2019, Denmark changed how psychiatric diagnoses are recorded in its famous National Patient Registry. Scientists worried this could ruin the data's reliability for research. This study investigated whether that change actually caused a problem.

Denmark's health registries are a treasure trove for global research. But in 2019, the system for outpatients changed dramatically:

  • Old System (DNPR2): An entire treatment course, regardless of the number of visits, was given a single main diagnosis at the end.
  • New System (DNPR3): Now, every single visit gets its own main diagnosis.

The concern was: Did this change create an artificial "jump" in the statistics, making it look like certain disorders suddenly increased?

ðŸ”¬ How Did They Check?

Researchers analyzed data from over 100,000 psychiatric patients in one region of Denmark from 2013 to 2021. They compared diagnostic trends in the year before the change to the year after.

ðŸ“Š Results: Good News for Science!

  • Although there were some statistically significant shifts, they were very small in absolute terms. For example, psychotic disorders (F2) and mood disorders (F3) only increased by 0.6%.
  • The number of different diagnoses a single patient received during a treatment course did not change substantially.
  • Even under the new system, clinicians are very consistent: over 90% of the time, the diagnosis from the first visit matches the diagnosis from the last visit.

âœ… Conclusion: Researchers Can Rest Assured

The study concludes that the administrative change in the registry system did not significantly destabilize the data. The Danish psychiatric registries remain a robust and reliable source for scientific research.

This finding is crucial to ensure the validity of hundreds of past, present, and future studies that rely on this data to better understand mental disorders.

Source: Bernstorff et al., 2022. Acta Psychiatrica Scandinavica. DOI: 10.1111/acps.13463
