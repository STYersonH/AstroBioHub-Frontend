In short: Colistin is a last-resort antibiotic for severe pneumonia, but its intravenous (IV) version is toxic to the kidneys. This study investigated whether delivering colistin directly to the lungs (nebulized) instead of IV is an effective and safer strategy for ICU patients.

ðŸŽ¯ The Problem: Hospital-Acquired Pneumonia and Kidneys at Risk

In the Intensive Care Unit (ICU), pneumonia caused by superbugs resistant to common antibiotics (CR-GNB) is a major threat. Treatment with IV colistin is an option, but the risk of severe kidney damage (nephrotoxicity) is very high, especially in patients who are already critically ill.

ðŸ”¬ The Question and the Study

Could we avoid IV colistin and instead use only the nebulized (inhaled) version along with other, less toxic IV antibiotics? To answer this, researchers reviewed the records of 557 ICU patients with this type of pneumonia. They compared those who received nebulized colistin as a substitute with those who did not.

ðŸ“Š Key Findings: Nebulization Works!

  • Patients who received nebulized colistin had a significantly lower rate of clinical failure. In other words, their treatment was more successful at 7, 14, and 28 days.
  • Even after adjusting for patient severity, the use of nebulized colistin was an independent factor associated with a better treatment outcome.
  • Safety: There was no difference in the occurrence of new kidney problems requiring dialysis, suggesting that the nebulized strategy is much safer for the kidneys.
  • Interestingly, no significant differences in outcomes were found between administering a high or low dose of nebulized colistin.

âœ… Conclusion: A Valid Substitutive Strategy

For critically ill patients in the ICU with superbug pneumonia, using nebulized colistin instead of the intravenous version (in combination with other antibiotics) is a strategy associated with better clinical outcomes and is safer.

This approach allows the infection to be targeted directly in the lungs without exposing the entire body, and especially the kidneys, to the toxic effects of IV colistin.

Source: Feng et al., 2023. Annals of Intensive Care. DOI: 10.1186/s13613-022-01088-4
