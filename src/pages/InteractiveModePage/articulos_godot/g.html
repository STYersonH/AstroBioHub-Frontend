Today's electronics use an electron's charge (on/off). Spintronics aims to also use its "spin" (up/down), which could lead to much faster and more powerful devices. The big problem is that an electron's spin "forgets" its direction very quickly. The goal is to control how long that spin memory lasts.

  • They created a special material with ultra-thin layers of Indium Gallium Nitride (InGaN/GaN). This structure is known as a "quantum well."
  • Inside this material, two opposing forces that normally erase spin memory almost perfectly cancelled each other out.
  • They used ultrafast lasers to measure how long the electrons "remembered" their spin direction (spin relaxation time).
  • Then, they applied mechanical strain (stretching the material) to see if they could disrupt this balance.
