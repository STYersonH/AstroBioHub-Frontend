Have you ever wondered what happens to our bodies in space? To find out, scientists sent a group of brave mice on a 30-day space mission aboard the Bion-M 1 satellite. This is the story of their journey and what we learned thanks to them.

üéØ What Was the Mission's Goal?

After a 16-year hiatus, Russia launched the Bion-M 1 satellite in 2013. The main goal was to study how the lack of gravity (microgravity) affects the body at a cellular and molecular level. Scientists wanted to see the changes in the muscles, bones, heart, blood vessels, and the nervous system.

üê≠ Preparing the "Astro-Mice"

Not just any mouse could go to space. Male mice of the C57/BL6 strain were selected. The biggest challenge was that male mice tend to fight, so they had to undergo special training:

  • Coexistence: Groups of three mice were formed and learned to live together peacefully in small spaces.
  • Special Diet: They were trained to eat a special food with a paste-like texture that provided all the nutrients and water they needed.
  • Final Selection: Only the healthiest, calmest, and most adaptable mice were chosen for the journey.

üõ∞Ô∏è Life Aboard the Satellite

The mice lived in automated cylindrical habitats. One of the most interesting observations was seeing how their behavior changed: on Earth, they moved around on the habitat floor. In space, without gravity, they were constantly seen clinging to the feeder wall, looking for a foothold.

üåç The Return to Earth and the Results

After 30 days in orbit, the capsule with the mice landed in the steppes of Kazakhstan. Upon their return, the animals showed clear signs of being unadapted to gravity: they were weak, disoriented, and had difficulty standing and walking.

Unfortunately, not all the mice survived due to a failure in the feeding system. However, scientists noticed something very positive: there were no fights among them. The coexistence training had worked perfectly!

üî¨ What Did We Learn from this Mission?

Despite the technical issues, the Bion-M 1 mission was a success. The returning mice provided valuable data on how life in space causes muscle atrophy, bone weakness, and balance problems. This information is crucial for protecting the health of astronauts on future long-duration missions, like a trip to Mars.

In summary, these little "astro-mice" made a great sacrifice that helps us better understand the challenges of space exploration and prepare for the future.
